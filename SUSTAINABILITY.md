# Sustainability Strategy for BOSC Community Library

## Sustainability Challenge

BOSC Community Library's sustainability challenge is not only financial. The project must remain legally open, technically maintained, pedagogically useful, and trusted by public institutions over time. Public-sector educational repositories often begin with grant funding or a course project, but they can decay if no one is responsible for link verification, license review, localization, contributor onboarding, accessibility, and long-term hosting. BOSC therefore needs a sustainability model that can support routine maintenance while protecting community ownership.

Because BOSC is licensed under GPL-3.0, commercial participation is possible, but downstream distributed improvements must remain open. This makes the project suitable for a mixed ecosystem of public agencies, universities, civic technology groups, librarians, and service vendors. The core question is whether BOSC should be sustained primarily through a Red Hat style service-based model or through a foundation-driven governance model.

## Model Comparison

| Dimension | Red Hat Service-Based Model | Foundation-Driven Model |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Revenue Mechanism | Paid support, implementation, training, integrations, certification, and managed services. | Membership dues, grants, donations, sponsorships, public funding, and shared infrastructure support. |
| Governance Fit | Efficient when a lead vendor has strong engineering capacity and public-sector procurement access. | Strong fit when legitimacy, neutrality, and multi-institution participation matter. |
| Community Implications | Can professionalize support quickly, but contributors may perceive the vendor as too dominant. | Encourages shared ownership and broader participation, but decisions may move more slowly. |
| Risk Profile | Revenue may depend heavily on one company or consultancy pipeline. | Funding may be fragmented and require active grant writing or sponsor relations. |
| Public-Sector Trust | Works well if procurement rules favor service contracts and source access is guaranteed. | Often easier to present as neutral public infrastructure rather than a vendor product. |
| Long-Term Maintenance | Strong if service contracts fund maintainers directly. | Strong if the foundation funds maintainers, documentation, events, and working groups. |

## Red Hat Service-Based Model

The Red Hat model treats open-source software as the shared foundation and sells value-added services around it. For BOSC, this could include deployment support for ministries, teacher training, data migration, custom localization, curriculum alignment, accessibility audits, and long-term maintenance agreements. This model is attractive because it creates direct revenue for the work that public agencies actually need. A ministry may not want to edit Markdown files or configure hosting; it may want a reliable partner that can implement the system, train staff, and respond when problems arise.

The risk is concentration of influence. If one vendor becomes the primary source of funding, the roadmap may drift toward paying clients rather than community needs. GPL-3.0 reduces the risk of proprietary capture, but it does not automatically guarantee healthy governance. BOSC would still need transparent decision-making, public issue tracking, and a clear maintainer process.

## Foundation-Driven Model

A foundation-driven model resembles the approach used by organizations such as the Apache Software Foundation or Linux Foundation. A neutral foundation can host governance, accept donations, coordinate working groups, publish standards, and build trust among ministries, universities, libraries, and civil society partners. For BOSC, this model supports the project's public mission because it separates stewardship from any single vendor.

The foundation model also fits education because resource quality depends on broad participation. Teachers can review curriculum relevance, librarians can improve metadata, universities can support research and translation, and government agencies can fund national adoption without owning the project outright. The main risk is operational overhead: foundations require governance documents, fiscal administration, meeting discipline, and active fundraising.

## Recommendation

BOSC should adopt a foundation-driven model with room for service vendors in the ecosystem. This hybrid recommendation best matches the project's public-sector identity. A neutral foundation or university-hosted steering committee should steward the roadmap, trademarks, contributor rules, and public accountability. Vendors can still sell GPL-compliant implementation, support, hosting, and training services, but they should operate around the project rather than above it.

This model protects trust. Ministries of Education are more likely to adopt BOSC if they see that the project is governed as public infrastructure rather than as a single company's sales channel. It also supports sustainability because funding can come from multiple sources: government grants, donor agencies, university partnerships, library associations, and service contracts. The result is a balanced model in which the commons remains open, the community retains voice, and professional support remains available for institutions that need it.
