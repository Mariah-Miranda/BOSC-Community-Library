# Legal Analysis: GPL-3.0 for BOSC Community Library

## Why GPL-3.0 Is Superior for a Public-Sector Project

The GNU General Public License version 3.0 is an especially strong legal foundation for BOSC Community Library because the project is framed as a public-sector educational resource. Public-sector software is not merely a technical asset; it is part of the institutional infrastructure through which citizens receive services, access knowledge, and evaluate public decision-making. A permissive license may allow reuse, but GPL-3.0 more directly protects the public's continuing right to inspect, modify, share, and improve software that is developed for public benefit.

For a government-aligned education project, transparency is not optional. Citizens, educators, librarians, and civil society organizations should be able to inspect how resources are selected, how metadata is organized, and whether access rules treat communities fairly. GPL-3.0 advances this objective by ensuring that distributed modified versions remain available under the same license. Its copyleft structure prevents a vendor from taking publicly funded work, improving it privately, and redistributing a closed derivative back to the public. This aligns with open government principles: public money should create public value, and publicly useful software should remain auditable and reusable.

GPL-3.0 also supports democratic participation. A school district, local library, university, or civic technology group may need to adapt BOSC for local languages, accessibility standards, rural bandwidth constraints, or national curriculum requirements. Under GPL-3.0, those users are not dependent on a single vendor's permission. They receive source code rights alongside redistribution rights, which makes local adaptation legally secure.

## Patent Grants and Trademark Protections Compared with MIT and Apache 2.0

GPL-3.0 is stronger than the MIT License on patent issues because MIT is brief and does not contain an express patent grant. MIT grants broad permission to use, copy, modify, merge, publish, distribute, sublicense, and sell the software, but it does not clearly address what happens if a contributor later asserts patents against downstream users. GPL-3.0 addresses this directly in Section 11, which provides a patent license from contributors for their essential patent claims. It also contains protections against discriminatory patent arrangements, reflecting the license's concern that patent deals should not undermine user freedoms.

Apache License 2.0 also contains an explicit patent grant in Section 3 and includes a patent termination mechanism when a licensee initiates patent litigation. In that respect, Apache 2.0 is more precise than MIT and broadly comparable to GPL-3.0 for patent clarity. However, Apache 2.0 is permissive rather than copyleft. It protects recipients from many patent risks, but it does not require distributed derivatives to remain open source. GPL-3.0 combines patent protection with a reciprocal sharing obligation, which is more appropriate when the public policy goal is not only reuse but also preservation of public access.

Trademark protection is different from copyright licensing. GPL-3.0 Section 7 permits additional terms that decline to grant rights under trademark law for use of trade names, trademarks, or service marks. This means BOSC can license its code freely while still protecting the project's name, public identity, and institutional reputation. Apache 2.0 similarly states in Section 6 that it does not grant permission to use a licensor's trademarks except as needed to describe origin. MIT does not expressly discuss trademarks, so trademark rights remain reserved by default under general law, but the absence of explicit language can be less educational for contributors and public administrators.

## Implications for a Commercial Paid Version

A commercial entity may build a paid version of BOSC Community Library, but GPL-3.0 shapes how that business may operate. If the company distributes a modified version, GPL-3.0 requires it to provide the corresponding source code for that distributed version under GPL-3.0. The company may charge money for distribution, installation, support, training, hosting, customization, or certified releases, but it may not convert distributed GPL-covered derivative work into proprietary software.

The practical consequence is that a vendor can sell services around BOSC, but improvements to the distributed software must remain available to recipients under the same license. For a public-sector project, this is a feature rather than a defect: it prevents public agencies from becoming dependent on closed forks of a public educational platform. A vendor that adds accessibility improvements, localization features, or curriculum integrations and then distributes the modified application must provide the corresponding source to its customers.

Several commercial strategies remain viable. A company can offer paid implementation, migration, curriculum mapping, training, compliance audits, custom integrations, and long-term maintenance. The project owner may also consider dual licensing if it holds the relevant copyrights or receives contributor license agreements, allowing one GPL version and one separately negotiated proprietary license. Another important limitation is the so-called SaaS loophole: GPL-3.0 generally triggers source obligations on distribution, not ordinary network use. A company that only runs a modified BOSC service for users over the internet may not be required by GPL-3.0 to release its server-side changes. The Affero GPL would close that network-use gap, but this project uses GPL-3.0 to balance strong public sharing norms with compatibility for a wider contributor base.

Overall, GPL-3.0 is the strongest fit for BOSC because it joins legal freedom with public accountability. It allows commercial participation while preserving the civic principle that publicly useful educational infrastructure should remain inspectable, modifiable, and shareable.
